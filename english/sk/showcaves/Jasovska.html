History

12th century first mentions on the Premonstrate Order in Jasov.
1452 oldest inscription in the cave.
1846 first opened to the public by Alojz Richter, an abbot of Jasov Monastery.
1878 first archaeological research by J. Nyari.
1916 first systematical exploration by geologist and paleontologist T. Kormos.
1923-24 explored, surveyed and first plan of the cave by V. Bluma and J. Zikmund.
20-JUL-1924 reopened after modern development.
1924-25 extensive archaeological research by Prof. J. Eisner.
1926 electric light installed.
1931 new artificial entrance created and cave tour extended by the Club of Czechoslovak Tourists.
1955 archaeological research by V. Lozek.
1962 geomorphological research by A. Droppa.
1978 archaeological rescue investigation because of a modification of the entrance part.
1995 start of speleotherapy in the cave.

Description

Jasovská Jaskyna (Jasov Cave) is one of the oldest show caves in the world. It is situated near the town of the same name on the eastern edge of the Slovak karst. It is located in the northern foothills of the Jasov plateau, in a rocky cliff known as the Maiden’s Rock at an altitude of 256 m asl. It was formed on five levels by the Bodva River in the limestone of the Middle Triassic Gutenstein Dolomites and the Steinalm Limestones and Dolomites.

The cave is famous for its archaeological finds, which date from the Palaeolithic or Old Stone Age and which include bones of the cave bear or Ursus spelaeus and cave hyaena, Crocuta spelaea. Finds also date from the Neolithic or New Stone Age when the cave was inhabited by early man. There have been rich finds from the Bükk culture, about 6400 to 6100 BC, including pottery and bones. The Bronze Age, Hallstatt and Roman Periods to the Middle Ages are also well represented. During Tatar and Turkish raids it was used by the local population as a refuge. In the Hussite Hall there is a charcoal inscription dating from the year 1452 which describes the victory of the "Brethren’s Armies of Jan Juskra of Brandys".

Seventeen species of bat are found in the cave. The Greater Horseshoe Bat (Rhinolophus ferrum equinum) is the most prolific followed by the Lesser Horseshose Bat (Rhinolophus hipposideros). This cave is regarded as the most important winter habitation site for bats in the whole of Slovakia.

The cave is noted for its abundance of speleothems which have been described as a "Wealth of Virgin Stalactites". The first chamber "The Old Hall" or Stary Dom contains curtain like stalactites and gigantic stalagmites. But the characteristic styles of decoration are the plum-like stalactites and the mighty pagodas of various shapes and colours. An unusually fine impression is given from the beauty of the "Great Dome or Velky Dom with rich speleothems known as the Great Waterfall, the Gallery, the Palm Grove, and the Weeping Willow, respectively. All this is overshadowed by the depth of the colour of the water in The Deep Lake. This is one of the largest chambers in the cave formed at the junction of the "Bat’s House" or Dom Netopierov and the Maze.

This is one of the best known but least publicised caves in Slovakia.


Text by Tony Oldham (2002). With kind permission.

Jasovská Jaskyna (Jasov Cave) is a rather strange show cave, and said to be one of the most important caves of the Slovak Karst National Park. The cave has a spectacular array of speleothems, mostly stalactites and stalagmites, but the main sight are the hundreds of inscriptions. Located on the most eastern rim of the Slovak Karst, in the Medzev Upland close to the eastern edge of the Jasovská Plateau, it was named after the nearby town Jasov.

Near the Jasov Cave lies a Premonstratensian monastery. According to an old legend, the cave was discovered by a monk who dared to take a look into the mysterious underground while collecting medicinal herbs. The first written mention of this story is from the 12th century, so it was known over centuries, and used as a hideout in times of Tatar or Turkish invasions. Both the monks and the local peasants found refuge here. The monks opened the cave to the public in 1846 after some modifications to allow easier navigation. This was officially ordered by the abbot Alojza Richtera (Alois Richter). This makes it the oldest show cave in Slovakia and one of the oldest show caves in the world. But it seems the cave was closed or at least became quite unpopular after only a few years. It seems the cave was gated and forgotten for a long time. But in 1923 the cave was first speleologically explored, surveyed and the first plan of the cave drawn by V. Bluma and J. Zikmund. At the same time the trails were repaired, and the show cave reopened in 1924 by the Section for Research of the Eastern-Slovakian Karst. Development went on, and a few years later electric light was installed, and in 1931 an artificial entrance was build and the tour extended by the Club of Czechoslovak Tourists.

There were several excavations in the cave. Although it was forgotten for a long time, it was known to Prehistoric man and of course to animals. Several excavations revealed the bones of the cave bear (Ursus spelaeus) and cave hyena (Crocuta spelaea). There was a massive accumulation of cave bear bones in one passage, which was then dubbed Bear Corridor. Human remains were found from the Neolithic (Bukk-Mountain Culture), Bronze Age, Hallstadt Age (Older Iron Age) and Roman Age.

The excavated items are partly on display in the weird entrance building. But the most spectacular archaeological remains can be seen in full in the cave: the hundreds of inscriptions which are located in the chambers at the end of the tour. As the cave is entered through an artificial entrance tunnel and exited through the natural entrance, those chambers are actually those closest to the entrance, although they are now the highlight at the end of the tour. The inscriptions and drawings on the cave walls are well preserved. The oldest inscription from 1452 records a victory of Jan Jiskra z Brandýsa. He was a supporter of King Sigismund of Hungary and fought with him against the Turks. But when the King died his underage son became King Ladislaus Postumus with his widow Elisabeth of Luxemboug reigning. The polish Jagiellonian Władysław was also crowned king and now the two were fighting for the crown. Jan Jiskra was successful for several years but finally in 1452, obviously after the victory mentioned in the inscription, he started to lose fights and was forced to leave the country.

However, as always the majority of the inscriptions are just the name of the visitor and the date of the visit. An inscription from 1576 in the Dóme netopierov (Bat’s Dome) witnesses that some lower parts of the cave had been known already in the 16th century. A dagger with chiseled ornamentation, probably dating from the time of the Turkish invasion, was found in the cave.
