History

1973 first discovered by Guangxi geologist Fu Zhongping who was in to Leye to conduct a geological survey.
1995 exploration of the tiankeng.
1998 existence first published by the Leye County Government and report by Guangxi TV.
2010 added to the UNESCO’s Global GeoPark List.

Description

大石围天坑 (Dàshí wéi tiān kēng, Dashiwei Tiankeng) is a huge doline, so huge it has a special name which is tiankeng. Sometimes tiankeng is translated sinkhole, but that’s only partly true. The term tiankeng has become an international scientific term for sinkholes which are at least 100 m diameter and 100 m deep. But this one is much bigger, the opening is 600 m by 420 m, and it is 613 m deep. Some say this is the largest tiankeng of the world. The area was declared a GeoPark to protect the karst features and developed with an outlook. It was developed with trails and a huge skywalk, a sort of half-bridge which allows a great view down into the sinkhole.

The tiankeng is its own ecosystem, separated from the outer world by 600 m high walls. The virgin forest at the bottom is home to rare species such as the flying squirrel. Below the edge of the breakdown pile on the tiankeng floor is the entrance of a downstream river cave.

The site became part of the Leye-Fengshan National Geopark, which became a UNESCO Global Geopark in 2010. The park includes much more than the tiankeng, there are also large subterranean rivers, natural bridges and extensive cave systems. It is surrounded by conical hills, a typical sign of cone or cockpit karst. The tiankeng is big enough to cut through three cones. The park includes a suite of large tiankeng and major cave systems which are only partly explored. Some are accessible by outlook, namely the Datuo Tiankeng and the Maoqi Dong.
