Description

The Catacombe di San Gennaro (Catacombs of St. Januarius) are located below the Basilica dell'Incoronata Madre del Buon Consiglio. San Gennaro is the patron saint of Naples. The catacombs were dug into a layer of 14,000 years old volcanic tuff, a yellow vulcanoclastite. They extend on two levels which are not superimposed, both characterized by much larger spaces than the more famous Roman catacombs. The tuff is rather soft, and so it was easy to dig caverns, no need to restrict the size overly. The chambers are up to 6 m high.

The catacombs were started in the 2nd century, probably as the tomb of a noble family, who then provided spaces for the Christian community. In the 4th century, following the burial of the remains of St. Agrippinus, the first patron of Naples, in the catacombs, the veneration started. The catacombs became an underground basilica dedicated to him. The main chamber is a single nave dug out of the tuff, which still preserves a bishop's chair carved into the rock. The altar has an opening, in which the faithful could see and touch the saint's tomb. The basilica has frescoes from the 5th century and is part of the Sacred Mile that goes from the tomb of San Gennaro to its treasure.

As we already mentioned, there are actually two catacombs, and it is unclear why they are united with the same name, as they are actually almost 200 m apart.
