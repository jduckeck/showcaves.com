Abric de Petracos, Castell de Castells Pintures Rupestres del Placita de Petracos 03793 Alicante Spain Rock paintings of the Pla de Petracos sanctuary The Pla de Petracos rock art site (Castell de Castells, Alicante) is located about 500 meters above sea level, on the left bank of the Barranc de Malafi. Discovered in 1980 by members of the Center d'Estudis Contestans, it is made up of eight coats of which five have perfectly visible painted motifs. The macroschematic realizations (8,000 years old) are well observed in four nearby shelters, highlighting the representation of the figure of the praying person due to its size and centered position. In front of it, a few meters from the rocky wall, a large stone stands out on the ground that could be related to the meaning of the pictorial representations. In a coat, separated from this ensemble, a small but exceptional Levantine Art motif (3,000 years old) was discovered: a wounded deer, which may have been part of a hunting scene of which only the prey has survived. It is a prehistoric manifestation chronologically subsequent to those of Macroesquematic Art. The Pla de Petracos is one of the best sites with rock art in the Valencian Community. At the time when the paintings were made, it was a sanctuary: a meeting and worship place for people united by deep beliefs, in which fertility and fecundity, the agricultural cycle or family ties took on a special role. The Pla de Petracos contains one of the main sites with rock art in the Valencian Community. Easily accessible, your visit allows you to enjoy a natural environment while getting closer to artistic productions typical of the beginning of the Neolithic Age. From a platform, different representations of Macroesquematic Art can be seen in four shelters arranged on the rocky pediment, with a person praying as the main figure in the center. In order to understand the different motifs, different panels have been arranged behind the fencing in which the rubbings of the paintings are reproduced and their meaning is suggested. Separated, in another enclosure you can see a small deer that surely was part of a hunting scene characteristic of Levantine Art, a prehistoric artistic manifestation after the Macroesquematic Art. The best guarantee for this art to be preserved is that all visitors are aware of its importance and take up the responsibility of caring for and respecting a Heritage that, as a legacy of our ancestors, must reach future generations intact.
