Description

Grotte de la Madeleine is the only show cave, located directly at the Ardèche gorge, on the left side of the river. The cave has a parking lot on top of the plateau, at the Ardèche tourist road. From here a path leads down to the edge of the gorge, and then along the cliff to the entrance of the cave. From this path, the visitor has a very nice view on the Ardèche river, 200 meters below. This is the deepest and most impressive part of the gorge. Even if you do not want to visit the cave, the belvedere in front of the cave is accessible without restrictions and for free, and considered the most beautiful outlook of the gorge. Many people are canoeing down the Ardèche and visit the cave by walking up to the cave entrance.

The cave of the Madeleine is actually made up of two caves: the Lescure Cave and the Madeleine Cave, one above the other. They are connected by an artificial tunnel. The upper cave, the Lescure Cave, was known for many years and was used to mine phosphate.

The Madeleine Cave is larger, and only 50 meters above the river at the foot of the cliff. Located in the middle of the cliff face, its entrance was hard to reach. It was discovered in 1887 by a shepherd named Germain Rigaud who was searching for a lost goat. He found a candle in his pocket, so at he entered the cave with only a flickering candle for illumination. This is one of those things we generally discourage, because it's far too dangerous. Its unclear how far he went in, but he obviously registered the potential of his discovery and shared his discovery with the locals. Obviously nobody was brave enough to explore the cave.

It was half a century later, when the French cavers, who are quite famous today actually started exploration. Robert de Joly was the first to enter the cave in 1936, followed by André Glory in 1946, and Jean Trébuchon in 1952-53. The two-year exploration by Jean Trébuchon was very meticulous, and he also carried out the first survey and drew a map.
