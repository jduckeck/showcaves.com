History

1771 Abbé de Feller mentions the existence of caves in Rocherfort.
1822 Jean Kickx and Adolphe Quételet publish their description Relation d’un voyage fait à la grotte de Han au mois d’août 1822 (Relation of a voyage made to the Han Cave in August 1822) which mentions caves at Rochefort.
1865 cave discovered by Alphonse Collignon.
1870 cave opened to the public.
MAY-1888 visited by E. A. Martel.
DEC-1944 inhabitants of Rochefort hide in the cave from a battl in the village.
1991 discovery of new cave passages.
2003 tour extended by a visit to le Cataclysme (The Cataclysm).

Description

This cave has two names and a combo version, which is a little confusing. Until about ten years ago the cave was advertised as Grottes de Rochefort, but that’s just the location, it means "the cave at Rochefort". The actual name of the cave is Grotte de Lorette after the nearby chapel of Lorette, and that’s how it is named in scientific literature and the cadastre. It seems the two names sometimes caused confusion, and so they changed the name of the show cave officially to Grotte de Lorette, the signs at the road, the huge sign at the cave entrance, all were replaced, new brochures printed and so on. The sign now says Grotte de Lorette and in the second line Rochefort, which is not the name but the location. However, the name confusion has obviously not yet ended as now the names Grotte de Lorette, Grotte de Lorette-Rochefort, and Grottes de Rochefort are still used. We decided to change the name to Grotte de Lorette on showcaves.com but the file is still named Rochefort. The name change was only one point of a complete renovation of the show cave. It included the paths, the light, a new sound system, a new reception building with audiovisual displays, and a small museum showing the scientific work at this place.

The entrance area of the cave contained prehistoric remains, and is of some interest to archaeologists. The cave has two entrances, both are collapse dolines, and one is used as the entrance for the tour, the other as the exit. The ticket office and car park are on the opposite side of the road, and there is a cul-de-sac which leads to the namesake Chapelle de Lorette which offers a great view on the valley of the Lomme, a tributary of the Lesse. You should definitely do the short walk to the outlook, the chapel is not special, but the view is great.

The cave was discovered in 1865 by Alphonse Collignon. Caves have blocked sections, or openings which are too high above for the available equipment, or passages which are not easy to see, and so the history of a cave is typically a succession of explorations and new discoveries, and caves "grow" in length with each new discovery. Grotte de Lorette is quite exceptional, as despite all the efforts of various Belgian speleologists since the original discovery in 1865 until 1991 no new sections were discovered. In 1991 members of the Spéléo Club les Fistuleuses wanted to attempt a climb from the old Salle du Val d’Enfer to an emerging opening in the cave ceiling. They got permission and they succeeded. They discovered the Galerie Oueste (West Gallery), And there is an addition to this official history of the cave. There is a local legend how the cave was actually discovered.

One day a pig disappeared into the underground and its herdsman crawled into this opening to find the pig. This is how he also ended up in a cave chamber, which today bears the name "Salle du val d’enfer" (Hall of the Devil’s Valley).

There is another piece of history, which is actually not told by the guides at the show cave. In December 1944, at the end of World War II the city was the location of a battle. At this time D-Day was over and the German army was retreating, and the 2. Panzer-Lehr-Division closed in on Rocheford, slowed by the lack of fuel. They took control of the small town, while the Americans disengaged. Many inhabitants had already fled the town, the remaining now went to nearby caves, the Lorette cave, the Falizes cave and the Trou Maulin. Lorette was not accessible at that time, but the others had already been used as a hideout during the religious wars. The caves were not very comfortable, cold and humid, nothing to eat and only dripping water to eat. Some had brought potatoes and kohlrabi, which were eaten raw. A few day later the American 83rd Infantry Division made their return in force. After 48 hours of intense fighting, the German troops were defeated and withdrew towards Nassogne. And while the time in the caves was not comfortable, the people had survived a massive battle.

The tour goes down 540 steps to the 37 m high chamber called Salle du Sabbath (Sabbath Hall), which is the largest chamber of the cave, 65 m below surface. The huge chamber is 125 m long, 35 m high, and 65 m wide. Giving the width is a little weird, because the floor is quite steep, the chamber is like a sloping crevice, and the width can actually only be seen in the floor plan, not in the real hall. The chamber was named after a formation of stalagmites, which resemble a chief witch sitting in her chair looking down, where a group of other witches are forming a circle of witches. The name of the formation is "Witches’ Sabbath" and as a result the chamber was named Sabbath Hall. Here, the guide launches the Montgolfière, a small hot-air ballon to the accompanying light and sound show. The vanishing ballon gives a very good impression of the enormous size of the chamber. That’s actually one of the few Son-et-Lumiere shows we can fully recommend. The flight of the hot air balloon has tradition, it is done on every tour since more than a century. The Son-et-Lumiere is obviously much younger.

It seems there was another renovation lately, but again no exact date is given, and the museum was updated. New is also the documentary Videokarst about the cave, which is shown before the tour.

Although the cave is close to Han it is still rarely visited. This makes the experience of a tour here much friendlier. So if you do not need "attractions" like train ride and animal park, and massive crowds of visitors, this cavern is a much better choice. Some time ago it was managed by the Grotte de Han and there was even a combo ticket available. The two caves still have connections as the cave is still owned by the Domaine des Grottes de Han. But Grotte de Lorette is today managed by the non-profit Association de Gestion Touristique de Promotion des Grottes de Rochefort et du Site de Lorette (Tourist Management Association for the Promotion of the Caves of Rochefort and the Site of Lorette). During winter, when the cave is closed, visits by reservation for groups are still possible, but are handled by the Grotte de Han, which is open all year. The number of visitors is much fewer, which is a shame, but also a good thing because a cave visit here is not only more interesting, it is cheaper and far less crowded. Franz Lindenmayr guessed in his description about 10 % of the visitors of Han, which are half a million per year. We guess Lorette has 20,000/a to 30,000/a. If you are in the area and have only enough time to see one cave, we strongly recommend this cave, not the crowded Grotte de Han. The huge chamber and the hot air ballon are unique!
