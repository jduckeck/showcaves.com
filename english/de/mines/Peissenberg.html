Description

The Bergbaumuseum Peißenberg (Mining Museum Peißenberg) has three sections, the main building with museum, the adventure gallery and the exhibition hall. The former colliery house, the main building, houses the ticket office and a mining museum with 14 rooms. The geological diagrams explain the formation of the coal seams in the foothills of the Alps. Tools, shaft models and pit lamps illustrate the work of the miners. The original mining process using arduous manual labour is presented, as is the increasing modernisation of coal extraction. The mining surveying system is presented. Uniforms, numerous photos and personal documents give an impression of the miners' lives. Afterwards, the so-called Tiefstollen (deep tunnel) is visited with a guide to gain an insight into the miner's working world. Presumably the tunnel is lately called the experience tunnel because you can experience the atmosphere of mining. Back on the surface, you can still visit the Tiefstollenhalle (deep gallery hall), this exhibition hall houses the large equipment and a very interesting showroom with a replica of the coal face.

Mining at Hoher Peißenberg began as early as the 16th century, but only in small quantities. Coal mining in the foothills of the Alps began in 1837, in Peißenberg itself with the begin of the "Tiefstollen" in 1869. By 1923, 1.5 million tonnes had been mined here, and by the time the mine closed in March 1971, more than 32 million tonnes had been mined.

The so-called Bockerlbahn, on the other hand, is not a mining railway. Rather, it is a fully functional model railway on which you can even ride. The track length is 370 m, the gauge 7 1/4 inches. It is run by the Bockerlbahner Club and is open on Sundays between May and October at the same time as the mining museum.
