History

1520 depicted on the altarpiece of the Usterlingen church
1579 first written mention by Apian.
1937 declare a Natural Monument.
2002 awarded the official seal of quality "Bavaria's most beautiful geotopes".
2006 included in the list of Germany's 77 recognised national geotopes.

Description

The Wachsender Felsen (Growing Rock) in Usterling is also popularly known as St John's Rock because the entire village is named after St John the Baptist. At the foot of the rock there is a chapel wayside shrine with a wooden figure of St John the Baptist, above the rock there is St John's Chapel. And below, in the village near the Inn, there is the church of St John, in which the oldest depiction of the growing rock from around 1500 can be found on the late Gothic winged altar. The altar was painted by an unknown artist from Hans Leinberger's circle. The baptism of Jesus by John was relocated to the growing rock, which can be seen at the bottom left. The use of local places as a motif was quite common at the time. This probably resulted in a connection between St John the Baptist and the water for the believers. The fact that there are two places of worship at the channel is probably due to the fact that the spring water was said to have healing powers for the treatment of eye diseases. Usterling was a place of pilgrimage for centuries.

The Growing Rock is a particularly beautiful Steinerne Rinne (tufa runnel), a special form of growing rock. Although it is quite short, around 40 metres, it has a record height of 5 metres, but is only 70 to 120 cm wide. It is known as the "largest" tufa runnel, but what is meant is that it is the highest in Germany. And it gets two to three centimetres higher every year.

Tufa runnels are formed by the precipitation of rock on both banks of a small stream. This causes dams to form, and these become higher and higher, and soon the stream is flowing on a kind of natural wall that it has deposited itself. These forms are created at mineral-rich thermal springs, but also in karst when water saturated with limestone emerges from a small spring. The water quickly loses its carbon dioxide and can then no longer hold the calcium carbonate dissolved, the lime is deposited. Plants such as lichens, mosses and even ferns help to consume the carbon dioxide. And after a short time, the carbon dioxide content has adjusted to that in the atmosphere and the deposition and also the tufa runnel ends.

Tufa runnel are fragile, they do not survive cold ages. It is therefore clear that this channel has been formed since the end of the last glacial period. In fact, it is estimated to be only 5,000 years old, although the last glacial period ended at least 8,000 years ago. It is unknown whether no stream flowed before or whether an imaginable predecessor channel was destroyed. The spring outlet is located at a layer boundary between water-bearing gravel at the top and water-resistant marl at the bottom. These are rocks of the Upper Freshwater Molasse from the Tertiary period, which consist largely of limestone. To the north is the somewhat lower valley of the Inn, which is covered with river deposits, gravel and ground moraine from the Quaternary period.

The current form of the tufa runnel is, strictly speaking, artificial. One way of destroying such channels is frost in winter, which causes the water in the channel to freeze and damage the channel. This is why the water has been diverted in winter for centuries. Branches and leaves were also regularly removed. The reason was, of course, that the pilgrims were economically important. Today, maintenance has been taken over by the Dingolfing-Landau district nature conservation organisation.

The Growing Rock is located on the eastern edge of the village in the forest and is signposted. It can be reached on a short footpath from a small car park on Mamminger Straße, at the end of the village in the direction of Zulling.
