Stratigraphy


The crust of the Earth is composed of solid rock, which is influenced by internal forces (plate tectonics) and external forces. The crust is covered by water and air, both destroying and decomposing rocks in a process called erosion. But as those geologic processes are circles, nothing is added, nothing removed. The rocks are reduce to small pieces, gravel and then sand, which is sedimented and during a process called diagenesis again transformed into rock. Components which are solved by the water are precipitate, for example when the water evaporates.

The result of those processes are so called sedimentary rocks, which formed in stratas or layers. So stratigraphy, a composition of strata and graphein, the Greek word for to draw, means to draw or otherwise record the different stratas. So it is the sciences of stratified rocks, but also the science of earth history in terms of climate, as certain rocks and the way they were formed give us information about the time they were formed in. So there is a very close relation between stratigraphy and the Geologic Time Scale.

Stratigraphy is very important for speleology, as only certain rocks are soluble and thus karstified. And virtually all of them are sediments: limestone, dolomite, gypsum and salt. So the structure of the strata, the thickness of the layers and the nature of the neighbouring stratas influence the formation of caves.
