Mundus Subterraneus

Athanasius Kircher


The Jesuit priest and professor of mathematics, philosophy and oriental languages, Athanasius Kircher worked in Würzburg and Worms. In 1678 he published his most famous work, Mundus subterraneus (Subterranean World). The exact title of the work was:


Mundus subterraneus
Athanasii Kircheri e Soc. Jesu Mundus subterraneus, in XII libros digestus.
Amsterdam: Joannes Janssonius à Waesberge & Filios, 1678.

458 Blatt, pp. i-xx, 1-366, i-vi; i-viii, 1-507, i-ix; 26 Tafeln; 383x240 mm


The book describes a variety of underground things: Caves, volcanoes and springs. But the book also contains a compilation of the most famous giants. There is an illustration in the book that shows a kind of order of the biggest giants:

  • The ten-metre-high Sicilian Giant whose remains were recovered in a cave near Trapani in Sicily in the 14th century. The Italian poet Gioivanni Boccaccio attributed these finds to the giantPolyphemus known from the Odysseus saga.
  • The giant Gigas Mauritaniae.
  • As the third largest giant, Kircher mentioned the Giant of Lucerne (Gigas). His remains had come to light in 1577 after a storm near Reiden near Lake Lucerne under a felled oak tree. The Basel physician Felix Platter calculated a body length of 19 feet, i.e. more than five metres, for this giant. It was not until the end of the 19th century that an anatomist clearly identified the remaining bone remains as those of a mammoth.
  • Then the biblical Goliath.
