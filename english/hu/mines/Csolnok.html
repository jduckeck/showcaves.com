Geology

The coal deposit is named after the town Dorog (Drostdorf)


Description

Csolnoki Bányászklub Múzeum (Csolnok Mining Club Museum) is actually the museum operated by the Csolnok Mining Club. Like many such museums there is a non-profit organisation with many volunteers whoo invest many hours in the preservation of old mining equipment. That's actually not special, but to call it Club museum is, it sounds a little unfprofessional, which the canservative work of the club definitely is not. The museum is also called Rákóczi telep Bányász Múzeum (Rákóczi settlement Mining Museum)

The coal in the area was first discovered in 1781, and soon small amounts were mined. The whole mining became a notch more professional and in 1845 the first written contract on mining coal at Dorog was drawn up between the Capter of Esztergom and the colliery managers Ferenc Wasshuber and József János Jülke. Obviously at that time, there were already collieries, and subsequently many great engineers became involved in developing the Dorog mines. Some important names of this era are Vilmos Zsigmondy, the geologist Miksa Hantken, and the mining engineers Henrik Drasche and Sándor Schmidt. They made Dorog a major mining centre, connected by rail with Budapest and by canal with the Danube. The town flourished around the turn of the century and until World War I. In the interwar period, Budapest's factories and population needed more and more coal. As mining was increased, immigrant miners needed homes and Several housing colonies were built in the 1920s and 1930s. Transylvanian coalminers moved here after the Treaty of Trianon, their hometowns were Hungarian before the war but now they were Romanian.

There are many German names in the history of the town and the coal mines. The village was settled by various waves of German settlers after its complete destruction destroyed in the Ottoman invasion. As a result it was inhabited by people of both Hungarian and German descent. After World War II, when the Germans were the aggressors, the Germans were expelled, even if they actually lived in town since the 16th century.

During the socialist era, the city became a typical socialist town with prefabricated blocks of flats. The mines gradually closed, but unlike the western capitalist countries the coal mining was not closed due to economic reasons.
