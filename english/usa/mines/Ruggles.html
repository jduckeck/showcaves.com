History

1803 Mica first discovered in Grafton by Sam Ruggles.
5 July 1805 first land purchase by Sam Ruggles.
1806 purchase of adjoining property.
1810 purchase of adjoining property.
09-NOV-1825 first advertisement for mica ("sheet isinglass") in the Boston Semi-Weekly Advertiser.
1834 sold to his son George Haskell Ruggles.
1840 270kg to 320kg of mica mined annually.
1869 11 tons mined.
1874 owned and operated by J.W. Kelton.
JAN-1877 1630kg mica shipped in one month.
1896 abandoned amid bankruptcy and litigation.
1912 mining for feldspar.
1932 purchased by the Bon Ami Company who mined feldspar for their non-abrasive scouring powder and glass cleaner.
1960 mine sold to the Wahlstrom family.
1963 mine closed due to cheaper sources of mica from foreign suppliers.
1963 opened to the public.
2016 mine closed and put up for sale.
2018 an online petition to turn the mine into a New Hampshire state park.

Geology

The Littleton Formation which was formed during the Devonian, approximately 300Ma ago, is primarily mica schist, surrounding the Ruggles pegmatite. The Ruggles pegmatite is pretty huge, 500m long, 100m wide, and 75m deep.

In early Devonian the area was marine and sediments were deposited forming sandstone, siltstone, and shale. At the end of the Devonian the orogeny started, the rocks were folded, submerged, uplifted and metamorphized. The east-western compression created folds running north to south. Volcanism caused by the tectonic forces produced igneous rocks. The presence of volatiles during the crystallization permitted large crystals to grow.

The most impressive giant crystals here were books of mica, as large as 1.50m in diameter.


Description

Located on Isinglass Mountain near Grafton, the Ruggles Mine is not really a mine, it is more like a quarry with numerous underground passages. This is not a show mine, there are no tours and no guides. The quarry is a fossicking site, where visitors can collect their own gemstones. Typical minerals found here are mica, feldspar, beryl, and uranium. The walls of white quartz and feldspar contain over 150 different minerals.

The first thing discovered here was mica, a thin, translucent silicate which was of great value. Not the value of gemstones, but pretty useful for various things. It was used to cover religious objects during the Middle Ages, as it was much more transparent than glass. Mica is heat resistant and transparent, so it was used for the windows in wood stoves and whale-oil lamps. Mica was also resistant against salt water, so it was used in ships windows. Until today it is used to insulate electricity. As it does not conduct heat or electricity, Early electrical toasters had mica in them.

Mica was first mined by the discoverer Sam Ruggles (03-AUG-1770 to 27-MAY-1843). He did not tell anyone about his find, mined secretly with his family, packed the mica into wagons along with farm product sand and shipped it secretly to Portsmouth. There it was shipped to England, where it was sold without anyone at New Hampshire knowing. It is not clear why he was so paranoid, but one possible explanation is the ownership of the land. In the early 1800s land was claimed, not purchased, so he could claim a certain amount each year. Probably he wanted to keep the mine secret until he had claimed the entire mountain.
