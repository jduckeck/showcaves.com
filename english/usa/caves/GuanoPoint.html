History

1930s Bat Cave discovered by a passing boater.
1950s cave purchased by the U.S. Guano Corporation and cable car built.
1959 end of mining deposits exhausted.

Description

Guano Point is a cave lookout, and there is actually no cave, so this place actually does not offer any underground except for some subterranean history and a really nice view. So if you are already at the south rim and a history buff this place is probably worth a drive. Guano Point is a hill at the south rim of the Grand Canyon and it is accessible by a road which ends here. The road and the name are a result of guano mining in the Bat Cave Mine, which is located deep below in the Grand Canyon on the opposite side of the river. If you have good binoculars you can see the cave entrance.

Bat Cave was a cave deep below, almost at the bottom of Grand Canyon. It was discovered in the 1930s by a passing boater. In the 1930s there was still no industrial fertilizer, and guano was called the white gold. It was nitrogen-rich abd its importance for modern farming was enormous, its price too. There was an estimate of more than 100,000 tons of guano in the cave. As a result many people tried to loot this treasure in some way, but for 20 years any attempt failed.

The U.S. Guano Corporation bought the property and constructed a USD 3.5 million tramway system to extract it. The lower station was at the cave entrance, the upper station at a hill they leased from the Hualapai Tribe, which became known as Guano Point. The road to this hill was needed to transport the guano on trucks. The tramway had several towers, but the main span between the cave and the opposite side of the river was 2.3km wide and went up 760m. The mine seems to have been rather profitable and was operated until the guano was exhausted. Unfortunately the predicted 100,000 tons were actually only 1,000 tons.
